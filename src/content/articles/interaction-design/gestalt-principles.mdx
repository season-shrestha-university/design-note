Gestalt psychology suggests that the human eye sees objects in their entirety before perceiving their individual parts. In interaction design, these principles are critical for creating interfaces that feel intuitive and organized.

The Principle of Proximity dictates that elements placed close together are perceived as related. By simply adjusting padding and margin, a designer can group a label, input field, and helper text into a single cohesive unit. The Principle of Similarity states that items sharing visual characteristics (like color or shape) are perceived as belonging to the same group. This is why primary action buttons should all share the same distinct color across an entire application.

By applying Gestalt principles, designers reduce visual clutter. They eliminate the need for excessive lines, boxes, and explicit labels, allowing the user's brain to naturally and instantly understand the relationship between elements on the screen.
