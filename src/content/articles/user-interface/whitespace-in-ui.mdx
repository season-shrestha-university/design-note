In user interface design, the space between elements is just as important as the elements themselves. Whitespace, also known as negative space, refers to the unmarked areas of a design. It is the breathing room around text, images, and buttons. 

Inexperienced designers often view whitespace as wasted real estate, trying to cram as much information as possible into the viewport. However, without sufficient whitespace, an interface becomes cluttered, illegible, and overwhelming. Whitespace acts as visual glue and visual separator simultaneously. Micro-whitespace (the small gaps between lines of text) improves readability, while macro-whitespace (the large gaps between major layout sections) guides the user's eye and dictates the flow of the page.

Moreover, whitespace is intrinsically linked to luxury and sophistication in design. A layout with generous margins and padding feels calm, intentional, and premium. By giving content room to breathe, designers ensure that the user's attention is drawn exactly where it needs to go.
