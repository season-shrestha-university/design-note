A heuristic evaluation is a usability inspection method where evaluators examine an interface and judge its compliance with recognized usability principles (the "heuristics"). Unlike user testing, which observes how real people use the product, a heuristic evaluation relies on expert analysis.

The process typically involves 3 to 5 evaluators independently clicking through a prototype or live product. They document any friction points they encounter, categorizing them against Nielsen's 10 heuristics, such as "Visibility of system status" or "Error prevention." After the independent reviews, the evaluators aggregate their findings and assign severity ratings to prioritize fixes.

While incredibly valuable for catching glaring design flaws early in the development cycle, heuristic evaluations cannot uncover genuine user behavioral nuances. They are a powerful tool to clean up an interface before putting it in front of real users, ensuring you don't waste testing time on easily preventable errors.
