When planning a usability test, researchers face a critical choice: should the session be moderated or unmoderated? Both methods offer distinct advantages and serve different stages of the product lifecycle.

Moderated testing involves a researcher actively guiding a participant through tasks, either in person or via video call. This allows the moderator to probe into the participant's thoughts, asking "Why did you click there?" or "What did you expect to happen?" It is ideal for early-stage prototypes and complex workflows where understanding the user's mental model is paramount.

Unmoderated testing, on the other hand, relies on software to present tasks to participants who complete them independently on their own time. Because there is no researcher present, the data reflects more natural, uninfluenced behavior. It is highly scalable, allowing teams to gather results from dozens of users in a single afternoon. It is best suited for validating clear, straightforward tasks or performing A/B tests on live products.
