takeaways:
  - Avoid leading questions that accidentally influence the participant's answer.
  - Focus on past behavior rather than hypothetical future behavior.

User interviews are a foundational qualitative research method used to understand user needs, motivations, and pain points. However, conducting a good interview is much harder than simply reading a list of questions. The way a question is framed drastically alters the reliability of the answer.

The golden rule of user interviews is to ask open-ended, non-leading questions. Asking "Did you find the checkout process confusing?" implies that the process was confusing. A better approach is, "Can you describe your experience checking out?" This allows the participant to highlight whatever stood out to them, without bias.

Furthermore, humans are notoriously bad at predicting their future behavior. If you ask, "Would you use this feature?" most users will politely say yes. Instead, focus on the past. Asking, "Tell me about the last time you tried to solve this problem," yields concrete stories, revealing the actual tools they used and the real frustrations they encountered. In user research, past behavior is the most reliable predictor of future behavior.
